# Mental Health Trends Across Countries (2017–2023)

This project explores how mental health evolved across countries before, during, and after the COVID-19 pandemic. It focuses on differences between age groups and looks at how lifestyle and economic factors relate to mental health.

---

## Project Overview

The goal of this analysis is to better understand global mental health trends using data from multiple sources.

Main questions:

- How did depression and anxiety change during COVID-19?
- Were young people more affected than older adults?
- Do lifestyle habits (like spirituality or smartphone use) have an impact?
- Can economic factors explain differences between countries?

---

## Data Sources

- **GBD (Global Burden of Disease)** — mental health prevalence (2017–2023)
- **OWID (Our World in Data)** — mental health trends and economic indicators
- **Sapien Labs** — lifestyle data (spirituality, family bonds, smartphone age, diet)
- **World Bank** — GDP per capita and health spending

---

## Tools & Technologies

- **Python (Pandas, NumPy)** — data cleaning and analysis  
- **Plotly** — interactive charts  
- **Power BI** — dashboard creation  
- **Jupyter Notebook** — exploration and step-by-step analysis  

---

## Key Insights

### COVID-19 Impact
- Mental health issues increased by about **+19.8%** during the pandemic  
- Anxiety increased more than depression  
- Levels of anxiety are still higher than before COVID  

### Age Differences
- Young people (15–39) show higher rates than older adults (60+)  
- The gap between age groups increased after COVID  
- Anxiety is more common than depression among youth  

### Lifestyle Factors
- **Spirituality** shows a positive link with better mental health  
- **Family bonds** seem more important for older adults  
- **Later smartphone use** is linked to better mental health in youth  
- **Ultra-processed food consumption** is linked to worse outcomes  

### Economic Factors
- Weak or no clear link between mental health and GDP or unemployment  
- Higher-income countries sometimes report more cases (possibly due to better diagnosis)

## Conclusion

This project shows that COVID-19 had a clear impact on global mental health, especially on anxiety levels.

Young people were more affected than older adults, and their situation has not fully gone back to normal after the pandemic.

It also appears that lifestyle factors play an important role. Things like spirituality, smartphone habits, or social environment show more visible links with mental health than economic indicators like GDP or unemployment.

So overall, mental health cannot be explained only by economic conditions. Social and daily life factors also matter a lot.
